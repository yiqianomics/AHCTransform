AHC and CLR Transformations for Zero-Containing Compositions

This visualization compares the Arc-Hellinger Centered (AHC) transformation, a square-root geometric transformation defined on the closed simplex, with the Centered Log-Ratio (CLR) transformation, which requires strictly positive components.

The example uses \(p=3\) only for display because three-part compositions can be plotted directly. The mathematical definitions below are written for general \(p\).

Definitions

Mathematical definitions

Both transformations produce zero-sum coordinates, but they have different domains before any preprocessing is applied.

AHC transformation

For a composition \( \pi \in \Delta^{p-1} \), define \( q_i=\sqrt{\pi_i} \), \( q_0=p^{-1/2}\mathbf{1} \), \( c=q^\top q_0 \), and \( s=\sqrt{1-c^2} \). Then

\( z_{\mathrm{AHC}}(\pi)=\frac{\arcsin(s)}{s}(q-cq_0). \)

The expression is interpreted by continuity when \(s=0\). This case occurs at the uniform composition, where \(q=q_0\), and the transformed coordinate is defined as \(z_{\mathrm{AHC}}(\pi)=0\). The square-root map is defined when a component is exactly zero.

CLR after zero replacement

Define the zero-replaced and renormalized composition by

\[ \tilde{\pi}_i(\varepsilon)= \begin{cases} \varepsilon, & \pi_i=0,\\ \pi_i, & \pi_i>0, \end{cases} \qquad r_i(\pi;\varepsilon)= \frac{\tilde{\pi}_i(\varepsilon)} {\sum_{j=1}^{p}\tilde{\pi}_j(\varepsilon)}. \]

CLR is then defined by

\( \operatorname{clr}(\pi)_i=\log(\pi_i)-p^{-1}\sum_{j=1}^{p}\log(\pi_j). \)

\( z_{\mathrm{CLR}}(\pi;\varepsilon)=\operatorname{clr}\{r(\pi;\varepsilon)\}. \)

This requires \( \pi_i>0 \) for all components. When zeros are present, this visualization replaces zero components by \( \varepsilon \), renormalizes the composition, and then applies CLR. Only exact zero components are replaced in this demonstration; positive components are not thresholded.

Comparison

Transformation-level comparison

CLR remains a central log-ratio transformation for strictly positive compositions. The issue illustrated here is the additional zero-replacement step required when exact zeros are present.

Notes

What the visualization shows

Why AHC is defined at boundary compositions

AHC begins with the square-root representation \(q_i=\sqrt{\pi_i}\). Because the square-root map is defined at zero, compositions with exact zero components remain valid inputs. The subsequent centering step maps the square-root point to a zero-sum coordinate representation without introducing an \(\varepsilon\)-dependent preprocessing step.

Why CLR requires strictly positive components

CLR is based on logarithms of the compositional components. If \(\pi_i=0\), then \(\log(\pi_i)\) is undefined; as \(\pi_i\) approaches zero, the corresponding log term tends to \(-\infty\). Zero replacement makes CLR computable, but the transformed coordinates then depend on the replacement rule and on the value of \(\varepsilon\).

What this visualization focuses on

This page focuses on the transformation step. It shows that AHC can be evaluated directly at zero-containing compositions, whereas CLR requires zero replacement before transformation. It also shows how the CLR coordinates of zero-containing points can change as \(\varepsilon\) changes.

What this visualization does not claim

This visualization does not claim that AHC identifies structural zeros, distinguishes structural zeros from sampling zeros, or performs differential abundance testing by itself. Those tasks require additional modeling or downstream inference.
