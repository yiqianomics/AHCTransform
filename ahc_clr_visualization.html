Why AHC handles zeros gracefully
AHC maps each composition π to its square root q = √π on the positive orthant of the unit sphere. This is well-defined even when some components are zero — the zero simply maps to zero on the sphere. The subsequent centering onto the zero-sum plane is a smooth projection that introduces no singularity.
  • No pseudo-count needed — results are exact
  • Boundary points (with zeros) map to the boundary of the sphere
  • Transformation is stable and reproducible
⚠️
Why zeros break CLR
CLR requires taking log(πᵢ), which diverges to −∞ as πᵢ → 0. A pseudo-count ε is added before log-transformation, but this is arbitrary: the result changes dramatically depending on the chosen ε. Try dragging the ε slider above and watch the zero-containing points jump around in the CLR plot.
  • The choice of ε is subjective and affects results
  • Small ε pushes points to extreme coordinates
  • Downstream analyses (PCA, regression) are ε-dependent
